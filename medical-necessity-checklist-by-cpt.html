Understanding Medical Necessity in Medical Billing

In revenue cycle management (RCM) and medical billing, medical necessity is the single most critical factor determining claim approval or denial. Under major payers like Medicare, Medicaid, and commercial health insurance companies (including UHC, Aetna, Cigna, and Anthem BCBS), healthcare providers are required to prove that every service, diagnostic test, or surgical procedure rendered was clinically justified. If the patient's medical chart lacks documentation supporting the specific billing guidelines, the claim is typically denied using CARC codes like CO-50 (Non-covered services — not medically necessary) or CO-16 (Missing information).

What is Medical Necessity in Medical Billing?

Payers define medical necessity as healthcare services or supplies that are proper, reasonable, and necessary for the diagnosis or treatment of a medical condition. To satisfy these audits, claims documentation must follow several key components:

  • Consistency: The procedure code (CPT) must align logically with the primary diagnosis code (ICD-10-CM).
  • Standard of Care: The procedure must not be experimental or investigational, and it must follow established clinical guidelines.
  • Documentation Strength: The treating physician's clinical progress notes must clearly describe the patient's clinical history, physical examination, assessment, and medical decision-making (MDM) process.

Crucial Documentation Elements for High-Denial CPT Codes

Certain high-cost CPT codes trigger automatic medical necessity audits. Let's look at what is required to pass audits for common specialties:

1. Advanced Imaging (Radiology - CPT 72148, 70450)

For MRI and CT scans, payers routinely demand proof of conservative therapy before approving the scan. For instance, for a Lumbar Spine MRI (CPT 72148), clinical notes must document at least 6 weeks of conservative treatment (such as physical therapy, chiropractic treatment, or NSAID therapy) alongside physical exam findings of a focal neurological deficit.

2. Invasive Injections & Procedures (Orthopedics/Pain Management - CPT 20610)

Joint injections (such as arthrocentesis of the knee or shoulder) require detailed procedure reports. You must document the specific joint, laterality (using modifiers RT or LT), drug name, NDC code, dosage injected, and the patient's objective response to prior injections. Medicare typically restricts joint injections to a maximum of 3-4 times per year, spaced at least 90 days apart.

3. Evaluation & Management Visits (E&M - CPT 99214, 99215)

High-level office visits are heavily audited for upcoding. High-complexity E&M billing must document either a high level of medical decision-making (MDM) based on problems addressed, risk of patient management, and data reviewed, or meet strict cumulative time thresholds spent on the date of service.
