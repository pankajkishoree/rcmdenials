CO-146

CO-146 Denial Code: Claim Not Submitted Within Timely Filing

Last Updated: June 2026 | Category: Timely Filing

📋 What is CO-146 Denial Code?

CO-146 is a Timely Filing denial code indicating that the claim was not submitted within the payer's timely filing limit. This is one of the most common and difficult denials to overturn.

Each payer establishes specific filing deadlines that providers must meet. Claims submitted after these deadlines are subject to denial, and overturning these denials requires strong documentation of timely submission or evidence of payer-caused delays.

🔍 Root Causes of CO-146 Denials

⏰

Missed Filing Deadline

  • Claim submitted after payer's filing deadline
  • Internal delays in claim preparation and submission
  • Lack of tracking for filing deadlines
  • Staff turnover causing missed deadlines
  • Inadequate claims management workflow
🔄

Internal Processing Delays

  • Slow internal claim preparation processes
  • Delayed charge entry from clinical documentation
  • Bottlenecks in claim review and approval
  • Resource constraints during peak periods
  • Lack of automated claims submission workflows
📡

Clearinghouse Rejection

  • Claim rejected by clearinghouse and not resubmitted timely
  • Technical errors causing submission failures
  • Incomplete claim data causing rejection
  • Clearinghouse processing delays
  • EDI connectivity issues
🔁

Prior Denial Not Followed

  • Previously denied claim not appealed or resubmitted
  • Appeal deadline missed for prior denial
  • Claim correction not completed within filing window
  • Staff not trained on appeal and resubmission timelines
  • Tracking system failure for pending claims

✅ Step-by-Step Resolution Process

1

Determine Filing Deadline and Submission Dates

Identify the exact filing deadline per the payer's contract and the actual date the claim was submitted. Calculate the number of days between date of service and submission to confirm if the claim was timely.

💡 Pro Tip: Common timely filing limits are 90 days (Medicaid), 1 year (Medicare), and 90-180 days (commercial). Always verify payer-specific requirements.
2

Obtain Clearinghouse Submission Reports

Request clearinghouse submission reports with timestamps that prove when the claim was actually transmitted. These reports serve as critical evidence for timely filing appeals.

3

Check Prior Claim Submission History

Review whether any prior claims were submitted for the same service. A previously submitted claim creates a filing trail that may satisfy the timely filing requirement even if the current claim was submitted late.

4

Gather Proof of Timely Submission

Collect certified mail receipts, electronic submission confirmations, clearinghouse timestamps, or any other documentation that proves the claim was submitted within the filing window.

⚠️ Important: Without proof of timely submission, overturning a CO-146 denial is extremely difficult. Document everything from the start.
5

File Appeal with Proof of Timely Submission

Submit a formal appeal with all supporting documentation. Clearly reference the filing deadline, submission date, and attach evidence such as clearinghouse reports and electronic receipts.

6

Request Exception for Payer-Caused Delays

If the delay was caused by the payer (e.g., system errors, incorrect address, delayed eligibility response), request a filing exception with supporting evidence of the payer's role in the delay.

📞 What to Ask the Payer Representative

Essential Questions for CO-146 Denials:

  1. "What is the filing deadline for this plan?"
  2. "When was the claim received by the payer?"
  3. "Do you accept clearinghouse timestamps as proof of timely filing?"
  4. "Is there an exception process for late submissions?"
  5. "What proof is required to overturn a timely filing denial?"
  6. "Was there any payer-caused delay that contributed to this late submission?"
  7. "Can you provide the exact date the claim was received in your system?"
  8. "What is the appeal deadline for this denial?"
  9. "Are there any previous claims on file that establish a filing trail?"
  10. "May I have a reference number for this conversation?"

⚖️ Appeal Guidance

If you believe this denial was made in error, you have the right to appeal. Follow these steps:

  1. Review the denial thoroughly — Understand exactly why the claim was denied.
  2. Gather supporting evidence — Collect all medical records and billing documentation.
  3. Write a detailed appeal letter — Address each denial reason specifically.
  4. Submit within the deadline — Most payers require appeals within 30-180 days.
  5. Follow up regularly — Track your appeal status.

Essential Questions for Your Appeal:

  • "What specific documentation is needed to resolve this denial?"
  • "What is the appeal deadline for this denial?"
  • "Can this claim be corrected and resubmitted?"
  • "Is there a peer-to-peer review option available?"

💡 Prevention Strategies

Reduce CO-146 Denials with These Best Practices:

  • Track Filing Deadlines: Maintain a centralized system to track all payer filing deadlines
  • Automate Claims Submission: Implement automated workflows to submit claims promptly after service
  • Monitor Clearinghouse Rejections: Review and address clearinghouse rejections daily to prevent missed deadlines
  • Set Internal Deadlines: Establish internal filing deadlines well before payer deadlines
  • Train Staff on Timely Filing: Ensure all billing staff understand payer-specific filing requirements
  • Implement Filing Alerts: Set up automated alerts for approaching filing deadlines
  • Audit Pending Claims: Regularly audit pending claims to identify potential timely filing risks
  • Document Everything: Maintain detailed records of all claim submissions and correspondence
  • Use Certified Mail: When mailing paper claims, use certified mail for proof of delivery
  • Establish Escalation Procedures: Create clear escalation paths for claims approaching filing deadlines
