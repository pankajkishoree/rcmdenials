CO-182

CO-182 Denial Code: Services Not Provided by Qualified/Credentialed Provider

Last Updated: June 2026 | Category: Provider Qualification

📋 What is CO-182 Denial Code?

CO-182 is a denial code indicating that the services were not provided by a provider who meets the payer's credentialing or qualification requirements. Payment is adjusted because the rendering provider lacks the necessary credentials, certifications, or specialty qualifications required by the payer for the specific services billed.

This denial typically occurs when providers have not completed payer credentialing, their credentials have expired, or their specialty does not qualify them to perform the billed services.

🔍 Root Causes of CO-182 Denials

📝

Provider Not Credentialed

  • Provider hasn't completed payer credentialing
  • New provider not yet enrolled with payer
  • Credentialing application still pending
  • Provider working under different NPI
  • Group vs. individual credentialing issues
⏰

Expired Credentials

  • Provider's credentialing has lapsed
  • Re-credentialing not completed on time
  • License expiration not updated
  • Board certification expired
  • DEA registration lapsed
🏥

Specialty Mismatch

  • Provider's specialty doesn't qualify for the service
  • Services outside provider's scope of practice
  • Specialty-specific procedure restrictions
  • Taxonomy code mismatch
  • Provider not qualified for billed procedure
👥

Supervision Issues

  • Service requires supervision by qualified provider
  • Supervising provider not properly credentialed
  • Incident-to billing requirements not met
  • Collaborative agreement issues
  • Scope of practice violations

✅ Step-by-Step Resolution Process

1

Verify the Rendering Provider's Credentialing Status

Check the provider's credentialing status with the specific payer. Confirm whether the provider is enrolled, what their effective date is, and if there are any restrictions on their participation.

💡 Pro Tip: Use the payer's provider portal or CAQH ProView to quickly verify credentialing status across multiple payers.
2

Check if Credentialing Application is Pending

If the provider is not yet credentialed, determine the status of any pending credentialing application. Note expected completion dates and any outstanding requirements.

3

Confirm Specialty and Taxonomy Codes

Verify that the provider's specialty and taxonomy codes on file with the payer match the services being billed. Some procedures require specific specialty qualifications.

⚠️ Important: Even if credentialed, a provider may be denied if their specialty doesn't qualify them for the specific procedure billed.
4

Determine if Supervising Provider Can Be Identified

If the service was provided under supervision, identify the supervising provider and verify their credentialing status. Determine if incident-to billing requirements can be met.

5

Submit Appeal with Credentialing Documentation

If you believe the denial is incorrect, submit an appeal with supporting credentialing documentation, including copies of active licenses, certifications, and payer enrollment confirmation.

6

Initiate Credentialing Process if Needed

If the provider is not credentialed, initiate the credentialing process immediately. Track the application and expedite if possible to prevent future denials.

📞 What to Ask the Payer Representative

Essential Questions for CO-182 Denials:

  1. "Is this provider credentialed with your plan?"
  2. "What credentials are required for this service?"
  3. "Is there a pending credentialing application for this provider?"
  4. "Can retroactive credentialing be arranged to cover this claim?"
  5. "What is the provider's current credentialing status and effective date?"
  6. "Are there specialty-specific requirements for this procedure?"
  7. "What documentation is needed to verify provider qualifications?"
  8. "Is there an expedited credentialing process available?"
  9. "What is the timeline for credentialing approval?"
  10. "May I have a reference number for this conversation?"

⚖️ Appeal Guidance

If you believe this denial was made in error, you have the right to appeal. Follow these steps:

  1. Review the denial thoroughly — Understand exactly why the claim was denied.
  2. Gather supporting evidence — Collect all medical records and billing documentation.
  3. Write a detailed appeal letter — Address each denial reason specifically.
  4. Submit within the deadline — Most payers require appeals within 30-180 days.
  5. Follow up regularly — Track your appeal status.

Essential Questions for Your Appeal:

  • "What specific documentation is needed to resolve this denial?"
  • "What is the appeal deadline for this denial?"
  • "Can this claim be corrected and resubmitted?"
  • "Is there a peer-to-peer review option available?"

💡 Prevention Strategies

Reduce CO-182 Denials with These Best Practices:

  • Maintain Active Credentialing: Track credentialing expiration dates and renew well in advance
  • Verify Provider Status Regularly: Check provider credentialing status with all payers before billing
  • Use CAQH ProView: Maintain current provider profiles in CAQH for streamlined credentialing
  • Track Application Status: Monitor pending credentialing applications and follow up regularly
  • Verify Specialty Codes: Ensure provider taxonomy codes match the services being billed
  • Document Supervision Arrangements: Maintain clear documentation of supervisory relationships
  • Implement Credentialing Alerts: Set up system alerts for upcoming credentialing expirations
  • Review Payer Requirements: Stay informed about payer-specific credentialing requirements
  • Train Staff on Credentialing: Ensure billing staff understand provider qualification requirements
  • Conduct Regular Audits: Periodically audit provider credentialing status across all payers
