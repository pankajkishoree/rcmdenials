CO-27

CO-27 Denial Code: Expenses Incurred After Coverage Terminated

Last Updated: June 2026 | Category: Contractual Obligation | Priority: High

📋 What is CO-27 Denial Code?

CO-27 indicates that the services were provided after the patient's insurance coverage terminated. This means the patient was not eligible for benefits on the date of service because their policy had ended, been cancelled, or not yet begun.

This denial typically results in patient responsibility, but verification of coverage dates is critical before billing the patient.

🔍 Common Reasons for CO-27 Denials

📅

Coverage Termination

  • Patient lost job/employment ended
  • Non-payment of premiums
  • COBRA coverage expired
  • Dependent aged out (turned 26)
  • Divorce/separation (lost spouse coverage)
  • Medicare Part B not enrolled
⏰

Timing Issues

  • Service before coverage effective date
  • Service after termination date
  • Gap in coverage between plans
  • Retroactive termination by payer
  • Late enrollment not processed
❌

Verification Errors

  • Eligibility check showed active (but wasn't)
  • Patient provided expired insurance card
  • Wrong effective dates in system
  • Payer system error during verification
  • Retroactive termination not reflected
🔄

Plan Changes

  • Switched to different insurance
  • Changed from individual to group plan
  • Moved to Medicare from commercial
  • Changed employers
  • Marketplace plan not renewed

✅ How to Resolve CO-27 Denials

1

Verify Coverage Dates

Contact the payer to confirm the exact coverage termination date and reason.

Questions to Ask Payer:
  • "What was the coverage termination date?"
  • "What was the reason for termination?"
  • "Was this a retroactive termination?"
  • "When was the termination processed in your system?"
  • "Is there any possibility of retroactive reinstatement?"
2

Contact the Patient

Reach out to the patient to verify their insurance status and identify any other coverage.

3

Check for Other Coverage

Investigate if patient had other active insurance on the date of service.

  • Spouse's insurance
  • Parent's insurance (if under 26)
  • Medicare (if 65+)
  • Medicaid
  • New employer coverage
4

Determine if Appeal is Warranted

Decide whether to appeal based on the circumstances.

⚠️ Important: If you verified eligibility and it showed active, document this thoroughly. You may have grounds for appeal or payer liability.

📞 What to Ask the Payer

Essential Questions for CO-27 Denials:

  1. "What is the exact coverage termination date?"
  2. "What was the reason for coverage termination?"
  3. "Was this termination retroactive?"
  4. "When did you process the termination in your system?"
  5. "We verified eligibility on [date] and it showed active - why?"
  6. "Is there a grace period for premium payments?"
  7. "Can coverage be reinstated retroactively?"

📎 Required Documentation for Appeal

  • Original claim with all codes and modifiers
  • Complete medical records supporting medical necessity
  • Any prior authorization or precertification documentation
  • Provider-payer contract or fee schedule reference
  • Detailed appeal letter with clinical justification

💡 Prevention Strategies

  • Real-Time Eligibility: Verify coverage at every visit, not just new patients
  • Document Verification: Save all eligibility verification confirmations
  • Check Multiple Sources: Verify through payer portal AND phone when possible
  • Collect Updated Cards: Request current insurance card at each visit
  • Ask About Changes: Specifically ask if insurance has changed
  • Verify Effective Dates: Confirm both effective and termination dates
  • Patient Communication: Educate patients about notifying you of coverage changes

⚖️ Appeal Guidance for CO-27

If you believe this denial was made in error, you have the right to appeal. Follow these steps:

  1. Review the denial thoroughly — Understand exactly why the claim was denied and what documentation is missing.
  2. Gather supporting evidence — Collect all medical records, billing documentation, and any prior authorizations.
  3. Write a detailed appeal letter — Address each denial reason specifically with supporting evidence.
  4. Submit within the deadline — Most payers require appeals within 30-180 days. Check your specific payer's timeline.
  5. Follow up regularly — Track your appeal status and be prepared to provide additional information if requested.

Essential Questions for Your Appeal:

  • "Can you provide the specific contractual clause that applies to this denial?"
  • "What documentation would be sufficient to overturn this denial?"
  • "Is there a peer-to-peer review option available for this type of denial?"
  • "What is the exact appeals process and timeline for this denial type?"
  • "Can the denial reason be clarified in writing with specific reference numbers?"
